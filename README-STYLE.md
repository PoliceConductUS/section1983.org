# Article Style Guide

Audience: pro se readers under stress, often reading on a phone, often without legal training.

## Targets

- Primer pages: grade 7-9
- Process pages: grade 6-8
- Articles: grade 8-10
- Deep-dive doctrine pieces: grade 10-11 max

## Rules

- Lead with the answer in the first two paragraphs.
- Prefer short sentences. Most paragraphs should average 1-3 sentences.
- Use one idea per paragraph.
- Use concrete nouns and verbs before legal abstractions.
- Define legal terms once, then use them consistently.
- Put examples before theory whenever possible.
- Break long lists into bullets or numbered steps.
- Avoid rhetorical flourishes unless they clarify the stakes quickly.
- Cut throat-clearing and repeated emphasis.
- Replace abstract framing with practical questions:
  - "Who can I sue?"
  - "What do I need to prove?"
  - "What document do I file next?"
- Treat the reader as capable but overloaded.
- When referring to the original 1871 statute, name it as the `Civil Rights Act of 1871` the first time, followed by `also called the Ku Klux Klan Act`.

## Preferred voice

- direct
- plain
- specific
- unsentimental
- second person
- written as training, not commentary

## Article Metadata

Use article metadata to help the reader decide whether to start reading.

- Article pages should usually show the publish date and estimated reading time near the title.
- Keep reading time simple, like `8 min read`.
- Put reading time on the article page, not on article cards or index pages.
- Do not add extra metadata just to look busy. If it does not help the reader decide or orient themselves, leave it out.

## Reader Position

Write to the reader as `you`.

Do not default to:

- law-review voice
- abstract third-person explanation
- detached commentary about what "plaintiffs" or "litigants" do

The site should feel like it is training the reader to do the work.

That means articles should help the reader learn how to:

- spot viable claims and weak claims
- gather facts and records
- draft pleadings and declarations
- anticipate defenses
- decide whether they have a somewhat reasonable case

The goal is not to make the reader feel inspired. The goal is to make the reader more capable.

When possible, prefer:

- "You need to show..."
- "If you do not have these facts yet..."
- "Before you file, ask yourself..."
- "One of the first questions the judge is going to ask is..."
- "For your claim, look for..."

over:

- "Plaintiffs must show..."
- "Litigants often argue..."
- "Courts have held..."
- "This may constitute..."
- "The issue is whether the plaintiff plausibly alleged..."

Case law still matters, but it should usually be introduced in service of training the reader how to think and act.

## Goal-Oriented Framing

Write toward the reader's actual goal:

- identifying whether rights were violated
- identifying what facts strengthen or weaken the case
- anticipating what the judge will care about
- anticipating how the defense will try to reframe the facts
- seeing what evidence or records matter next

Prefer framing like:

- "One of the first questions the judge is going to ask is..."
- "For your claim, look for..."
- "What hurts this claim is..."
- "What makes this issue stronger is..."
- "The defense is going to say..."
- "The defense will try to reframe this as..."
- "The fight will usually be over..."

Avoid abstract doctrinal phrasing when a goal-oriented sentence would train the reader better.

Lead with the main point, not with the negative case.

Prefer:

- "If you sue someone who has absolute immunity, the defense will argue that this person cannot be sued for this kind of act."

Avoid openings like:

- "The defense is not really arguing..."
- "This is not really about..."
- "The court is not saying..."

Only lead with the negative case when the exception or contrast is the actual point of the paragraph.

For example:

- weaker: "The issue is whether you invoked clearly enough."
- stronger: "One of the first questions the judge is going to ask is whether you clearly told them you were invoking your right to remain silent."

## Plain-Language Rewrite Patterns

When editing an article, actively look for sentences that make the reader translate the point before they can use it.

These are common problem patterns:

- legal shorthand instead of practical effect
- long or abstract questions
- contrast-first sentences
- "not X" openings
- internal or lawyerly labels that the reader would not use in real life
- broad category words where a simpler everyday word would work

Use these rewrite patterns:

- shorter question instead of abstract formulation
  - weaker: "Is this the kind of defendant who can be sued for this kind of act at all?"
  - stronger: "Can this defendant be sued for this behavior at all?"

- positive lead instead of contrast-first setup
  - weaker: "This is not qualified immunity..."
  - stronger: "This article is about absolute immunity..."

- direct point instead of "not really" language
  - weaker: "The defense is not really arguing that nothing happened."
  - stronger: "The defense will argue that this person cannot be sued for this kind of act."

- everyday wording instead of internal labels
  - weaker: "reachable defendants"
  - stronger: "defendants you can properly include"

- practical outcome instead of procedural shorthand
  - weaker: "the court does not reach the facts"
  - stronger: "the court may dismiss the claim without deciding whether what happened to you was wrong"

- practical skill instead of abstract doctrine label
  - weaker: "The issue is whether you invoked clearly enough."
  - stronger: "One of the first questions the judge is going to ask is whether you clearly told them you were invoking your right to remain silent."

When you revise, ask:

- Would a stressed reader say this sentence out loud this way?
- Does the sentence lead with the point, or does it make the reader wait for the point?
- Can I swap a category word like `act`, `issue`, `capacity`, or `conduct` for a more concrete word like `behavior`, `role`, `decision`, or `what happened`?
- Does the sentence tell the reader what the judge or defense will actually do with the fact?

## Defense Reframing

Do not explain claims only from the plaintiff's perspective.

Train the reader to pressure-test the claim by asking:

- how will the defendant rename this issue?
- what facts will the defense emphasize?
- what facts will the defense minimize or ignore?
- what "reasonable officer" or "administrative" or "safety" story will they try to substitute for the reader's story?

Good articles should help the reader see both:

- what the judge will likely focus on
- how the defense will try to redefine the dispute before the judge sees it

Use explicit lines when helpful:

- "The defense will say this was not retaliation. They will say it was a lawful arrest supported by probable cause."
- "The defense will try to turn this from a coercion issue into a routine booking issue."
- "The defense will try to treat this as one bad incident, not a city policy problem."

The reader should come away knowing not just what their claim is, but what it will be called by the other side.

## Use Concrete Weak-vs-Strong Examples

When a doctrine turns on wording, timing, or framing, show the reader the difference with concrete examples.

Prefer:

- weak statement versus strong statement
- weak fact pattern versus strong fact pattern
- weak allegation versus usable allegation

Do not leave the reader with only abstract labels like:

- ambiguous
- conclusory
- coercive
- clearly established

Show what those words look like in practice.

## Define Functional Labels Before Using Them

Do not ask the reader to use a legal category before you have taught them how to recognize it.

If an article uses labels like:

- judicial act
- prosecutorial act
- investigative act
- legislative activity
- testimonial act
- administrative role

define them in plain language the first time they appear.

Prefer:

- "a judicial act, meaning something a judge normally does, like ruling, sentencing, or issuing an order"
- "a prosecutorial act, meaning something a prosecutor normally does as the government's lawyer, like filing charges or presenting the case"

Avoid dropping the label into a question or checklist before the article teaches what the label means.

Bad:

- "Was this a prosecutorial act?"

Better:

- "Was this a prosecutorial act, meaning something the prosecutor did as the government's lawyer in court?"

The reader should never have to guess what a category word means in order to answer the article's own questions.

## Translate Legal Effect Into Plain Outcome

When legal shorthand hides the practical result, rewrite the sentence in terms of what the judge actually does to the claim.

Prefer:

- "the court may dismiss the claim without deciding whether what happened to you was wrong"
- "the claim can be thrown out even if your facts are true"
- "the judge may end the claim before you get discovery"

over:

- "the court does not reach the facts"
- "the claim fails as a matter of law"
- "the plaintiff did not plausibly allege"
- "the issue is barred"

The reader should be able to understand the legal effect without already speaking lawyer.

## Avoid Absolutes

Absolute language is usually a credibility problem in legal writing.

Avoid words and phrases like:

- always
- never
- everyone
- nobody
- every lawyer
- all judges
- the only reason
- completely

Use narrower, more accurate phrasing instead:

- usually
- often
- in many cases
- in this kind of case
- one common reason
- one serious risk

Prefer:

- "This is a nightmare scenario for many civil rights lawyers."

Avoid:

- "This is the nightmare scenario for every civil rights lawyer."

If a sentence sounds absolute, ask whether you can prove it across the full range of cases. If not, narrow it.

## Audit Checklist For Existing Articles

When reviewing an existing article, check for these specific issues:

- openings that begin with what the article is not about instead of what it is about
- questions that are longer or more abstract than they need to be
- phrases the reader would have to mentally translate into normal speech
- sentences that explain doctrine without saying what the judge does next
- "check your understanding" prompts that test a different skill than the opening promised
- sections that introduce a contrast before the reader understands the main rule
- terms like `viable`, `plausible`, `barred`, `reframe`, `capacity`, `reach the facts`, or `economic viability` without a plain-language explanation close by
- overconfident absolutes like `always`, `never`, `everyone`, `nobody`, or `every lawyer`

When in doubt, simplify toward:

- what happened
- what the judge will ask
- what the defense will argue
- what facts help or hurt
- what the reader should do with their own record

## Scope Boundary

This site is for helping the reader identify violations, gather facts, understand claims and defenses, and build a civil case from events that have already happened.

It is not primarily a site about how to behave during a live police encounter.

That means articles should usually focus on:

- what happened
- what facts matter now
- what records or evidence to gather
- what claims or defenses those facts support
- how those facts affect pleading, motion practice, or settlement value

Avoid drifting into pre-arrest coaching or real-time encounter advice unless it is tightly connected to later claim analysis.

For example:

- good: "If you invoked, document exactly when, what words you used, and what happened next because those facts may matter to suppression, coercion, or due-process analysis."
- bad: "Here is how to manage the encounter so police do not question you."

The reader's main task on this site is not to optimize the encounter. It is to understand whether rights were violated and whether the record supports a somewhat reasonable case.

## Avoid

- long stacked clauses
- repeated moral framing before practical instruction
- unexplained jargon
- dramatic openings that delay the point
- paragraphs longer than about 90-120 words

## Article Series

Use a series when one topic is too large, too technical, or too strategic for one article to carry clearly.

Good candidates for a series:

- doctrines with multiple pleading paths, like Monell
- topics that split into distinct practical problems, like records, complaint drafting, and summary judgment
- areas where the reader needs both foundation and advanced follow-on guides
- topics where later articles would be confusing without a clear starting point

If a topic can be explained clearly in one article, do not force it into a series.

## Lead Page For A Series

If you create a series, the first page must work as the entry page for a stressed reader who may know nothing about the topic.

The lead page must do these things in this order:

1. explain what the thing is
2. explain why the reader should care
3. explain why it is hard, dangerous, or commonly misunderstood
4. explain when the topic is likely relevant to the reader's case
5. point the reader to the deeper follow-on guides

Do not open a lead page with strategy if the reader does not yet know the concept.

Do not assume the reader already knows:

- why this doctrine matters
- whether it applies to their case
- how it differs from related claims
- what problem the article is solving

## Follow-On Articles In A Series

Each follow-on article should solve one specific problem.

Examples:

- how to get the evidence
- how to plead the claim
- what facts survive dismissal
- what theories are strongest or weakest
- what usually fails and why

Do not make every article in the series a second overview. Each one should earn its place by answering a narrower question.

## Cross-Linking Rules For A Series

If a series exists:

- the lead page should link to the follow-on articles in a visible list
- each follow-on article should link back to the lead page
- follow-on articles may point to each other when the order matters
- links should tell the reader what to do next, not just name related pages

Prefer language like:

- "Start here if you are deciding whether this claim belongs in your case."
- "Read this next if you need the evidence before discovery."
- "Read this after the overview if you are drafting the complaint now."

Avoid vague link language that assumes the content hierarchy is obvious.

## Reader Foundation Check

Before publishing, ask:

- Does this article define the core concept before using it?
- Does it assume the reader knows something that only appears later on the site?
- Does it tell the reader whether they are in the right place?
- Does it distinguish this claim, motion, or doctrine from similar ones?
- Does it tell the reader what to read next and why?

If the answer is no, the article probably needs more foundation or clearer routing.

## Guest Contributor Rule

A guest author should be able to read only this style guide and understand:

- who the audience is
- what reading level to target
- whether they are writing a standalone article or a series lead page
- how much foundation to include before strategy
- how to route the reader to the next useful page

When in doubt, choose more explicit framing and more hand-holding over elegance.

## Standard Article Structure

Yes, most articles on this site should follow a common structure.

The goal is not rigid formatting for its own sake. The goal is to make sure the reader gets:

- orientation
- foundation
- the practical legal issue
- the facts that matter
- the next useful page

Use this default structure unless there is a strong reason not to.

## Required Sections For Most Articles

### 1. Opening Orientation

The first 1-3 paragraphs should answer:

- what this article is about
- why you should care
- where this issue usually shows up in a case

The opening should help the reader decide:

- "Am I in the right article?"

### 2. What This Is

Define the claim, doctrine, motion, stage, or evidence problem in plain language.

Do not assume the reader already knows the concept.

### 3. Why It Matters

Explain why this issue affects the reader's case.

This can include:

- whether it kills claims early
- whether it increases leverage
- whether it affects damages
- whether it changes who should be sued

### 3.5. Learning Objectives

Near the beginning of the article, tell the reader what they should be able to do after reading it.

Keep this practical and skill-based.

Good learning objectives usually start with verbs like:

- identify
- distinguish
- spot
- compare
- decide
- find

For example:

- "After reading this article, you should be able to identify which defendants absolute immunity will usually protect."
- "After reading this article, you should be able to spot the facts a lawyer will care about when deciding whether your case is economically viable."

These objectives should line up with the `Check Your Understanding` block at the end. The reader should feel like the end-of-article prompts are testing the same skills the article promised to teach.

### 4. What Facts Matter

Train the reader to spot the facts that strengthen or weaken the issue.

This is usually the most important part of the article.

### 5. Common Problems, Defenses, or Failure Points

Tell the reader where cases usually break down.

This section should make the reader more realistic and more analytical.

### 5.5. What The Defense Will Say

Most claim, doctrine, and drafting articles should include a short section that tells the reader how the defense will attack that specific issue.

This can be a separate heading or folded into `Common Problems, Defenses, or Failure Points`, but it should usually be explicit.

Train the reader to expect:

- how the defense will rename the issue
- what facts the defense will emphasize
- what facts the defense will minimize
- what early dismissal or summary-judgment argument the defense will likely try first

Good examples:

- "The defense will say this was a lawful arrest supported by probable cause, not retaliation."
- "The defense will say this is a thin complaint that groups all defendants together."
- "The defense will say this was one bad incident, not a city policy problem."

The point is not to make the article more cynical. The point is to train the reader to pressure-test the claim before the other side does it for them.

### 6. What To Look For In Your Own Record

This section routes the reader back to the actual work:

- video
- reports
- witnesses
- policies
- prior lawsuits
- medical records
- booking records
- dispatch logs

### 7. What To Read Next

Route the reader to the next useful page and tell them why.

## Optional Sections

Use these only when they materially help.

### Common Scenarios

Good for claim articles like false arrest, retaliation, excessive force.

### Key Defenses

Good when the doctrine is defense-heavy, like Heck, qualified immunity, arguable probable cause, or Monell dismissal.

### Common Defense Tactics

Good when the article needs to explain the recurring moves the defense will use across the whole case or across a full category of claims.

Examples:

- shrinking the facts
- renaming the conduct
- isolating the officer from the city
- minimizing damages
- turning missing records into speculation
- treating the plaintiff as the real problem

### Timeline Or Sequence

Good when timing changes the rule, like Miranda, invocation, or process-stage articles.

### Example Fact Patterns

Good when readers need concrete models to compare against their own facts.

### Practical Checklist

Good near the end of the article, especially for drafting or evidence-gathering pieces.

### Check Your Understanding

Use this near the end of an article when it helps the reader test whether they can actually apply the concept.

Do not use it as trivia.

The goal is to test:

- claim spotting
- defense spotting
- fact strength
- what the judge is likely to focus on
- what the reader should do next with their own record

The reader should not have to invent the framework from scratch.

That means the article must first teach:

- what question the judge is likely to ask
- how the defense is likely to reframe the issue
- what facts matter to that fight

Only then should the `Check Your Understanding` block ask the reader to apply that framework.

Prefer short prompts like:

- "What is the strongest claim here?"
- "What fact helps the defense most?"
- "What record or document would you want next?"

Prefer guided prompts over open-ended ones.

Better:

- "In this article, the first question the judge is likely to ask is whether the act was judicial or investigative. Which one fits your facts?"
- "The defense will try to call this routine booking rather than coercive questioning. What facts in your record cut against that?"

Worse:

- "What would the judge ask first?"
- "What would the defense try to reframe?"

Good `Check Your Understanding` blocks usually include 2-4 items:

1. one short scenario
2. one guided judge-or-defense framing question
3. one `apply this to your own case` prompt

When possible, give the reader a way to check their own answer.

The default pattern should be a native `<details>` / `<summary>` block after each question.

Why this pattern works:

- it works on desktop and mobile
- it does not require JavaScript
- it lets the reader try first and then check
- it keeps the page skimmable

Use short model answers:

- for scenario questions, give the most likely answer and one sentence explaining why
- for judge-or-defense framing questions, give the framing the article taught
- for own-case prompts, use `A strong answer would mention...` rather than pretending there is one exact correct answer

Keep the hidden answer short. It should usually be 1-3 sentences, not a second article hidden under the question.

Avoid school-style multiple choice unless the article is mainly about a narrow rule or exception.

## Structure By Article Type

### Claim Article

Use this general flow:

1. what the claim is
2. why it matters
3. what you usually need to show
4. what facts help
5. what the defense will say
6. how these claims usually fail
7. what to read next

### Doctrine Or Defense Article

Use this general flow:

1. what the doctrine is
2. when it applies
3. why it is dangerous or important
4. what facts trigger it
5. what the defense will say
6. common misunderstandings
7. how it affects your case
8. what to read next

### Strategy Or Case-System Article

Use this general flow:

1. what stage or pressure point this article is about
2. what the judge is likely to ask
3. what the defense is likely to do
4. the common tactics or filters
5. what facts or behavior help you
6. what facts or behavior hurt you
7. what to do next in your own case

### Drafting Article

Use this general flow:

1. what document or pleading this is
2. what job it does
3. the structure that usually works
4. how the defense will attack it
5. common drafting mistakes
6. what facts or exhibits strengthen it
7. what to compare it against
8. what to read next

### Evidence Or Records Article

Use this general flow:

1. what evidence you are trying to get or evaluate
2. why it matters legally
3. where to find it
4. what details matter most
5. what agencies or defendants will argue
6. how to use it later
7. what to read next

## Transitions Between Sections

Do not jump from concept to concept without setting up the reader.

Before moving into a denser section, make the transition explicit:

- "Now that you know what this claim is, the next question is what facts usually make it viable."
- "That leads to the main defense."
- "The timing matters because the rule changes depending on when this happened."

If a section feels like it starts in the middle of a thought, it probably needs a transition sentence.
