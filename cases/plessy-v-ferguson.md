## What Happened

Homer Plessy challenged a Louisiana law requiring separate railroad accommodations for white and Black passengers.

## What the Court Decided

The Supreme Court upheld the law and endorsed the "separate but equal" framework.

That decision became one of the most infamous precedents in American law because it gave constitutional cover to state-enforced segregation.

## What It Means in Practice

_Plessy_ matters here mostly as a reminder that even deeply entrenched Supreme Court doctrine can be wrong and can later be repudiated.

It was eventually rejected by [_Brown v. Board of Education_](/cases/brown-v-board-of-education).

## How You Can Use It

Use _Plessy_ when you need a stark example that the age of a precedent does not prove its legitimacy.

## How It Can Be Used Against You

Defendants may say _Plessy_ is such an extreme example that it does not tell you much about modern civil-rights procedure.

That is fair. Its value is not doctrinal detail. Its value is showing how badly the Court can get the law wrong and how long a bad rule can survive.
