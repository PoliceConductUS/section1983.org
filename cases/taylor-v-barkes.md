## What Happened

An inmate committed suicide shortly after entering a Delaware correctional facility. His estate sued under [§ 1983](/terms-and-concepts/section-1983), arguing that state officials had failed to use constitutionally adequate suicide-screening procedures.

The lower court denied qualified immunity to some defendants.

## What the Court Decided

The Supreme Court reversed.

The Court held that, even assuming the Constitution required some protection against inmate suicide, there was no clearly established law recognizing the specific right the plaintiff asserted: a right to proper implementation of adequate suicide-prevention protocols.

Because the law was not clearly established at that level of specificity, the officials got [qualified immunity](/terms-and-concepts/qualified-immunity).

## What It Means in Practice

_Taylor v. Barkes_ shows how qualified immunity can defeat a case even after a plaintiff alleges a serious constitutional failure with concrete facts.

The fight does not end when you show something went badly wrong. The court can still ask whether earlier case law clearly established the exact right at issue and dismiss if the answer is no.

## How You Can Use It

Use _Taylor v. Barkes_ when you need an example of how courts define the right at a very specific level before deciding whether qualified immunity applies.

It is also useful for explaining why a case can survive one stage and still die on qualified immunity.

## How It Can Be Used Against You

Defendants will cite _Taylor v. Barkes_ to argue that even obvious institutional failures do not create liability unless earlier precedent clearly put officials on notice about the specific conduct.

That is why plaintiffs have to research clearly established law early and frame the right carefully.
