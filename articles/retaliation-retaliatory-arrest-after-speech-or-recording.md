If the officer arrested you right after you criticized police, questioned them, or recorded them, you may be thinking: this was retaliation.

Sometimes that is exactly right. But retaliatory-arrest claims are harder than they look because [probable cause](/terms-and-concepts/probable-cause) often becomes the central fight.

This guide is about the core retaliatory-arrest claim itself: what the claim is, what facts matter most, and how timing, officer statements, and comparator evidence can help.

## What the claim is

A First Amendment retaliation claim says the government took adverse action against you because of protected speech or expressive activity.

In a police case, that usually means:

- criticizing an officer
- questioning police conduct
- recording police in public
- refusing to stop protected speech

## What you usually need to show

In simple terms, you are trying to show:

1. you engaged in protected speech or expressive conduct
2. the officer took adverse action against you
3. the speech was a substantial reason for that action

In an arrest case, the timing often matters a lot. If the arrest came right after your criticism or recording, that can support the inference of retaliation. So can officer statements showing irritation, anger, mockery, or hostility toward your speech.

## Facts that help

These facts often matter most:

- what you said
- whether you were recording
- what the officer said before the arrest
- whether the officer threatened arrest before any supposed offense appeared
- whether video captures the sequence
- whether others engaged in similar conduct without being arrested

## What to watch for

Retaliation claims are often strongest when the record shows:

- protected speech first
- arrest second
- no meaningful change in your behavior in between
- no real offense other than the speech itself

That sequence is the backbone of the claim.
